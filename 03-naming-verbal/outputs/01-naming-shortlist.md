# 01 — Naming Shortlist

**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** 2026-04-19
**Owner:** Agent 3 (Naming & Verbal Identity)

---

## How to read this document

Each candidate is presented with honest tradeoffs — not a pre-decided recommendation disguised as a shortlist. Names are grouped by register so stakeholders have genuinely different options, not eight variations on one theme.

**Registers covered:**
- A. Plain-functional — does what it says, no subtext required
- B. Cricket-cultural institutional — cricket reference that isn't a cliché
- C. Institutional / double-meaning — works in both cricket and general English
- D. Contextual / modern — adjacent to the game without naming a playing term

**Cricket terms explicitly avoided (per BRIEFING.md):** Wicket, Stumps, Googly, Howzat, Maiden, Crease, Yorker, Silly, Slip.

## Register A — Plain-functional

### 1. FixtureDesk

**Etymology / meaning**
Compound of "fixture" (canonical product term, see `shared/GLOSSARY.md`) and "desk" (institutional service point — help desk, front desk, service desk). Plain English. No subtext needed.

**Strengths**
- Communicates purpose immediately — no brand explanation required
- "Fixture" is already the canonical term across all product language; using it in the name creates consistency
- "Desk" evokes professional service infrastructure — not consumer, not startup
- Works in B2B contexts (clubs, administrators, league secretaries)
- Domain as compound has reasonable availability prospects (see `02-availability-checks.md`)

**Weaknesses**
- "Desk" carries 2010s SaaS product naming associations (Zendesk, FreshDesk, Intercom) — may date the product
- Reads more as an internal tool than a network of clubs
- The compound requires two words to say — minor but real cost in word-of-mouth
- Less warmth than Register B options

**Specific risks**
- Not a cricket cliché
- UK "fixture desk" as a phrase has some usage in local authority and events contexts — not the same domain, but check
- Monogram FD is clean but not distinctive

**Day-one discoverability**
High. "Fixture" is a searchable cricket term. Users who know what they want will find this; cold users won't land on it.

---

### 2. ClubRecord

**Etymology / meaning**
"Club" (cricket club, the primary organisation unit) + "record" (the record-keeping tradition in cricket — scorebooks, match cards, fixture sheets).

**Strengths**
- Direct and functional; no interpretation required
- "Record" evokes the scorebook tradition — something every cricket club values
- Works well in administrative / club governance contexts
- Low cliché risk

**Weaknesses**
- May read as a statistics or data product rather than a coordination tool
- Less memorable; feels like a description rather than a name
- "Club" is generic across all sports; no cricket-specific signal
- "Record" could imply recording achievements rather than arranging fixtures

**Specific risks**
- Not a cricket cliché; low trademark risk
- Sounds slightly database-flavoured

**Day-one discoverability**
Moderate. "Club" is too broad; "record" doesn't surface cricket coordination.

## Register B — Cricket-cultural institutional

### 3. Pavilion

**Etymology / meaning**
From French *pavillon* (tent, banner). In cricket, the pavilion is the clubhouse building at the centre of every ground: where dressing rooms are located, where tea is taken, where the scorecards are posted, where the fixture secretary sits, and where the club's social and administrative life happens. The pavilion is not a playing term — it is the institutional heart of the club.

**Strengths**
- Universally understood by cricketers without triggering cliché: the pavilion is the place cricket is *organised*, not just played
- Registers immediately with the target audience (fixture secretaries, captains, club admins)
- Warm without being sentimental; institutional without being cold
- One word; easy to spell; unambiguous pronunciation
- Evokes the right comparison points: National Trust, a well-run county club's website
- The logo and visual identity has strong architectural reference points (pavilion silhouettes are distinctive)
- Metaphorically coherent: the platform is where clubs come when they're organising, not when they're playing

**Weaknesses**
- Doesn't telegraph "fixture management" or "coordination" — relies on brand to do that work
- Single common English word; .co.uk and .com almost certainly taken by hospitality/events/venue businesses
- Could be confused for a venue-booking or clubhouse product
- "Pavilion" as a word is used by hospitality venues, shopping centres, sports facilities — brand noise exists

**Specific risks**
- Not a cricket cliché (playing terms are the clichés; the pavilion is architecture)
- Domain strategy requires a suffix: pavilioncc.co.uk, getpavilion.co.uk, pavilionapp.co.uk — one of these should be available
- Veers toward nostalgic *if* visual identity goes in a heritage direction — this is a brand (Agent 5) risk, not a name risk

**Day-one discoverability**
Low unassisted. Cricket context makes it clear; cold users won't find it via search. This is acceptable — the audience is acquired through cricket channels, not cold search.

---

### 4. The Square

**Etymology / meaning**
In cricket, "the square" is the prepared area at the centre of every ground — the groundsman's most carefully maintained territory, where all playing pitches are cut. It is the literal and metaphorical centre of cricket operations. The term is cricket-literate without being a playing term or a batting/bowling cliché.

**Strengths**
- Cricket-knowledgeable audiences will recognise it immediately
- Evokes both the geographical centre of a cricket ground and a "square deal" — fairness, the level playing field
- Institutional; not flashy; slightly abstract
- Suggests that coordination happens at the centre, not the periphery

**Weaknesses**
- "The" in a product name creates practical awkwardness (URL, App Store listing, in-sentence reference: "I posted it on The Square" vs "on Square")
- Non-cricketers won't get the reference — low outside-cricket discoverability
- "Square" has too many other meanings (town square, mathematical, payment processor Square)
- Payment processor Square (Squareup) creates real brand noise risk
- Hard to shorten or nickname meaningfully

**Specific risks**
- Proximity to Square (the payments company) is a genuine risk — trademark sanity check required
- "The" makes domain registration awkward (thesquare.co.uk likely taken by venues)

**Day-one discoverability**
Low. Cricket-literate audiences will get it; general audiences won't. The Square payments brand creates search noise.

## Register C — Institutional / double-meaning

### 5. Groundwork

**Etymology / meaning**
English compound with two simultaneous meanings: (1) the preparatory work before a project — "laying the groundwork" — and (2) the work that happens on and around a cricket ground. Evokes preparation, reliability, and the unglamorous-but-essential business of arranging a season.

**Strengths**
- Metaphorically very coherent: the product is exactly "the groundwork" of a cricket season
- Institutional register; no startup energy
- Non-cliché cricket reference; doesn't name a playing term
- Subtly reinforces the trust/reliability theme (reliable people do the groundwork)

**Weaknesses**
- **Critical risk:** Groundwork UK is a large, established environmental and community charity with strong brand recognition across the UK. "Groundwork" as a brand name in the UK carries that charity association — the risk of confusion is real.
- Domain (groundwork.co.uk, groundwork.com) almost certainly taken by Groundwork UK
- Could read as an NGO or community development product, not a coordination tool

**Specific risks**
- Groundwork UK charity is a serious brand conflict — not a legal bar, but a significant perception problem
- Flag explicitly: this name is de-risked only if Groundwork UK is not well-known in the cricket club demographic (unlikely — they operate in community sport)

**Day-one discoverability**
The charity association would generate confused search traffic.

### 6. Arrangement

**Etymology / meaning**
Drawn from cricket's own informal vocabulary. In cricket, a "friendly arrangement" between clubs is the standard way fixture secretaries describe a non-league fixture: "We've made an arrangement with Blanktown CC for the 15th." The word captures the informal-but-reliable nature of the product.

**Strengths**
- Deeply cricket-literate without any cliché; insiders will recognise it immediately
- Elegant single-word name
- "Making an arrangement" is exactly what the product enables
- Formal enough for institutional use; warm enough to not be cold

**Weaknesses**
- Low product-name legibility — does not read as a digital product at first glance
- Weak SEO: "arrangement" covers flowers, music, legal agreements — extreme polysemy
- Target users who aren't already steeped in fixture secretary culture may find it opaque
- Domain (arrangement.co.uk, arrangement.com) almost certainly taken as common English words

**Specific risks**
- Not a cliché; not a playing term
- May feel too soft/formal for a product that needs to feel trustworthy and direct

**Day-one discoverability**
Very low. No cricket signal; many competing meanings.

## Register D — Contextual / modern

### 7. Pitchside

**Etymology / meaning**
Compound: "pitch" (the playing surface in cricket; also used in football/rugby) + "side" (beside, adjacent). Evokes being present at the ground, coordinating from the boundary — not in the game, but making it possible.

**Strengths**
- Evokes proximity to cricket without naming a playing term
- Modern compound without startup energy
- Clean syllable count; easy to say
- "Pitch" is not on the banned list; "pitchside" as a compound is distinct from "Pitchly"-style vowel soup

**Weaknesses**
- "Pitch" is not cricket-exclusive — primary UK sports association may lean football, especially among general users
- Cricket players more commonly reference the "square" or "strip" than the "pitch" in ground-level conversation — "pitch" is understood but slightly less insider
- "Side" as suffix is directional rather than purposeful — doesn't suggest coordination or administration

**Specific risks**
- Football association creates dilution risk
- Not a cricket cliché; reasonable domain availability prospects

**Day-one discoverability**
Moderate. "Pitch" is sport-generic; "pitchside" is clean. Works with cricket branding applied.

---

### 8. Fieldwork

**Etymology / meaning**
English compound: "field" (the cricket field, the outfield) + "work" (the practical, unglamorous, essential work of coordination). Also: "fieldwork" in general English means practical research or investigation in the real world — the work done outside an office, on the ground.

**Strengths**
- Double meaning holds: the practical work of arranging fixtures, done in the field (of cricket)
- Institutional; not startup-y; not nostalgic
- "Work" suffix emphasises the utility of the product — this is for people who want to get things done
- Clean, two-syllable compound

**Weaknesses**
- "Field" is less cricket-specific than "pitch" or "ground" — fieldwork is an academic/research word first
- The academic/research association may dominate: users may think of anthropology or journalism before cricket
- Less immediately legible as a cricket or coordination product

**Specific risks**
- No cricket cliché risk
- Domain "fieldwork.co.uk" / "fieldwork.com" likely taken (common compound used by research agencies)

**Day-one discoverability**
Low cricket signal. The research/academic meaning dominates in general search.
