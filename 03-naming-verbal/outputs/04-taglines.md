## How to use this document

Taglines are presented with context on where each works best and which register it occupies. These are working options for stakeholder discussion — not a pre-decided recommendation. The chosen name affects which tagline lands: the options are written for **Pavilion** as primary and noted where they also work for FixtureDesk.

Constraints applied (from BRIEFING.md and Agent 1 voice guide):
- No "seamless", "effortless", "powered by"
- No "Made for cricket clubs" — generic
- Nothing that promises what the product cannot deliver
- No exclamation marks
- No Silicon Valley construction ("the platform for…", "the future of…")
- Matches: calm, direct, institutional, cricket-literate

---

## Option 1: Find your fixture.

**Register:** Plain-functional, verb-forward
**When to use:** Product header; app store primary tagline; onboarding headline
**Works with:** Both Pavilion and FixtureDesk

**Notes:**
This is the product's primary action, stated directly. Uses the canonical verb ("find") and the canonical noun ("fixture") from `shared/GLOSSARY.md`. No metaphor required. The period makes it a statement, not a prompt. A fixture manager reads this and immediately understands the product.

**Why it works:** It doesn't explain the product — it states the action. The audience already knows what a fixture is; the tagline respects that knowledge.

**Risks:** It is very functional; it does no brand or emotional work. On its own as the only communication, it undersells the trust and coordination aspects. Best when the name (Pavilion) is doing the institutional/cultural work.

---

## Option 2: Arrange your season.

**Register:** Cricket-literate, action-oriented
**When to use:** Marketing materials; homepage subheading; email subject lines in onboarding campaigns
**Works with:** Pavilion (strongly); FixtureDesk (adequately)

**Notes:**
"Arrange" is the verb cricket fixture secretaries use: "We've made an arrangement." "Can you arrange a game?" It is cricket-literate without being insider. "Season" grounds it in the annual rhythm of amateur cricket — one season at a time, not a one-off transaction. The tagline covers the full scope of the product (not just finding one game, but organising a season) without over-promising.

**Risks:** "Arrange" is soft; it does not signal reliability or the trust dimension. Does not surface "cricket" explicitly — context-dependent.

---

## Option 3: For clubs that get the game organised.

**Register:** Institutional, audience-defining
**When to use:** Marketing materials; About page; partnership communications with cricket boards
**Works with:** Both names

**Notes:**
This is not for a header — it's too long. But as a positioning statement in context, it works precisely because it defines the audience without flattering them. "Get the game organised" is exactly what fixture managers do; they don't call it "coordinating fixtures" or "finding opponents." They call it getting the game organised. It respects the culture.

**Risks:** Too long for primary use. The word "clubs" is correct (see GLOSSARY.md) but may read as excluding individual service providers. "That" instead of "who" for clubs is technically correct (organisations, not people) but may read as slightly cold.

---

## Option 4: The fixture desk for amateur cricket.

**Register:** Direct, descriptive, functional
**When to use:** App store description subheading; secondary header for new-user onboarding; SEO page title context
**Works with:** Primarily FixtureDesk (as a descriptor); works as a functional strapline for Pavilion too

**Notes:**
This is a functional description, not a tagline. But in the right context (app store, search result snippet) it does more work than a poetic tagline: it tells a user exactly what this is. "Amateur cricket" is the right qualifier — not "grassroots", not "recreational", not "non-professional." Amateur cricket is what the audience calls their game.

**Risks:** If the product name is FixtureDesk, this tagline is redundant (the name already says "fixture desk"). Works better as a descriptor sentence than a primary tagline.

---

## Option 5: Reliable opponents. Reliable officials. Reliable clubs.

**Register:** Trust-forward, institutional
**When to use:** Brand/marketing context; homepage value proposition; investor materials; partnership introductions
**Works with:** Both names

**Notes:**
Repeating "reliable" three times makes the trust dimension impossible to miss, without using the word "trust" (which is internal language per the glossary) or "rating" (forbidden). The pattern — opponent, official, club — mirrors the product's coverage: finding opposition, finding support, building trust. Each sentence covers a distinct product area.

**Risks:** This is a bold structural choice for a product that values understatement. Three-part repetition is more rhythmically prominent than the calm, direct voice usually calls for. Use with care — it works as a paragraph opener, not a header. Could also read as too aspirational before the product has established its reliability.

## Recommendation (for stakeholder discussion)

For the **Pavilion** name, pair *Find your fixture.* as the primary tagline (does the functional work) and *Arrange your season.* as the secondary marketing tagline (does the cricket-literate brand work). These two at different sizes and contexts give the product a complete register: direct + literate.

For the **FixtureDesk** name, pair *Arrange your season.* as the primary tagline (the name already does the functional work; the tagline can afford to be more cultural).

These pairings are suggestions for Agent 8 (Content & Microcopy) to test against real product contexts.
